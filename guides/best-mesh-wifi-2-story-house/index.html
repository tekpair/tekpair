Why single routers fail in 2-story homes

The most common Wi-Fi complaint we hear — and we hear it constantly in homes across Cohoes, Troy, and the Capital Region — is that the upstairs bedrooms have terrible signal. The router is downstairs, signal is great near the TV, and by the time you get to the second floor it's borderline useless.

This isn't your ISP's fault. It's physics. Wi-Fi signal degrades with distance, and it degrades faster when it has to pass through floors, load-bearing walls, and the air gaps inside them. A 2-story house with 2,400 sq ft puts roughly 35–45 feet of diagonal distance between a downstairs router and an upstairs bedroom — plus a floor with joists, insulation, and wiring in between. That's a lot for a single radio to push through.

The other problem: router antennas are typically designed to broadcast outward in a flat disc pattern, not upward. A router sitting on a shelf radiates most of its signal horizontally. Very little goes up through the ceiling to the floor above.

The fix isn't a faster router — it's more radios in more places. That's what a mesh system does. Each node acts as a full router with its own radio, handing your devices off seamlessly as you move through the house.

What to look for in a mesh system

Wi-Fi standard: 6 vs. 6E

Wi-Fi 6 (802.11ax) is the current mainstream standard and is more than fast enough for most 2-story homes. Wi-Fi 6E adds a 6 GHz band — useful if you have several newer devices (iPhones from 2022+, recent laptops) and want the extra headroom. For most households, Wi-Fi 6 is the sweet spot between cost and capability.

Backhaul: dedicated vs. shared

Cheap Wi-Fi extenders cut your bandwidth in half because they use the same radio to talk to the router and to your devices. True mesh systems use a dedicated backhaul channel — a separate radio that handles node-to-node traffic only. This keeps client speeds high even when you're far from the main node. Look for "tri-band" or "dedicated backhaul" in the specs.

Node count for a 2-story house

Most 2-story homes between 1,800–3,000 sq ft do fine with 2 nodes. One goes near your modem (usually downstairs), one goes in a central upstairs location. For homes over 3,000 sq ft, or with unusual layouts — L-shaped floor plans, thick plaster walls, a detached garage — plan for 3.

App quality matters more than you think

You'll use the app to set up, manage devices, run speed tests, and troubleshoot. Eero's app is the easiest. Orbi's is functional but dated. TP-Link Deco's is solid. Netgear Nighthawk Mesh is the worst of the bunch — avoid it if you hate confusing interfaces.

Setup tips for 2-story homes

We've set up mesh systems in hundreds of homes. Here's what we actually do on the job to get the best coverage.

Node placement

  • Primary node near the modem. Your main node needs to be close to where the coax or ethernet enters the house. Don't stretch that run — keep the modem-to-router connection short and wired.
  • Second node in a central upstairs location. A hallway between bedrooms is ideal. Avoid placing it in a bedroom that's off to one side — you want it to cover the whole floor.
  • Don't put nodes in closets or cabinets. Signal doesn't pass well through enclosed spaces. A node on a shelf in the open works dramatically better than one hidden behind books or in a media cabinet.
  • Keep nodes 30–50 feet apart. Closer isn't always better — nodes need some distance to form an efficient mesh. Directly above/below each other between floors is a good target.

Hardwiring nodes (ethernet backhaul)

If you have ethernet runs in the walls — or you're willing to run one — connecting nodes by wire instead of wirelessly nearly doubles performance. All the systems on this list support ethernet backhaul. If you have a coax outlet upstairs, you can also use MoCA adapters to create a wired backhaul without pulling new cable.

Wired backhaul is the single biggest upgrade you can make. It eliminates the wireless backhaul bottleneck entirely and is worth the extra effort if you can manage it.

Disable your ISP's router Wi-Fi

Running your mesh system behind your ISP's router creates a double-NAT situation that causes subtle problems — slower speeds, devices that can't see each other, game lag. You have two options:

  • Call your ISP and ask them to put your modem in bridge mode (disables its router/Wi-Fi functions)
  • Or log in to the ISP router's admin page and disable the Wi-Fi — then connect your mesh primary node to it via ethernet
