Why your router can't cover two floors

The most common Wi-Fi complaint we hear — and we hear it constantly — is that the upstairs is dead. The router is downstairs. Signal is great near the TV. By the time you get to the bedroom upstairs, you're crawling along on two bars and pages won't load.

Here's what's actually happening: Wi-Fi signal weakens with distance, and it weakens a lot when it has to push through floors. A typical two-story house puts 35–45 feet of diagonal distance between your downstairs router and an upstairs bedroom — plus a floor full of wood, insulation, and wiring in between. That's a lot to push a signal through.

There's also a geometry problem. Most routers spread their signal outward in a flat disc shape — like ripples in water. They're not designed to push signal up through a ceiling. So the second floor isn't just far away, it's in a direction the router wasn't designed to cover well.

The fix isn't a faster router — it's more radios in more places. Mesh systems put a full radio on each floor. Each node talks to the others, and your phone or laptop connects to whichever one is closest. No manual switching, no dead zones.

What actually matters when buying a mesh system

Wi-Fi 6 vs. Wi-Fi 6E — what's the difference?

Wi-Fi 6 is the current standard. It's fast, stable, and supported by every device made in the last few years. Wi-Fi 6E adds a third band (6 GHz) that's less crowded and faster for compatible devices. If you have newer phones or laptops (2022 and up), 6E is a nice upgrade. If most of your devices are a few years old, you won't notice the difference. Either way, both are great upgrades over what most people currently have.

What is "backhaul" and why does it matter?

When your mesh nodes talk to each other — passing your traffic between nodes — that communication is called the backhaul. Cheap Wi-Fi extenders use the same band for both their "talk to the router" job and their "talk to your devices" job. This cuts your speed in half or worse.

True mesh systems have a dedicated backhaul channel — a separate radio used only for node-to-node traffic. Your devices get their own dedicated lanes. This is why good mesh systems feel fast everywhere, while cheap extenders feel slow in the rooms they're supposed to help.

How many nodes do you need?

For a typical two-story house between 1,800–3,000 sq ft: 2 nodes is almost always enough. One downstairs near your modem, one upstairs in a central spot. Buy a 2-pack first — you can always add a third later if a corner of the house still has weak signal. All the systems we recommend let you expand.

How to set it up for the best coverage

Where you place your nodes matters just as much as which system you buy. Here's what we actually do on every home installation.

1

Primary node goes near the modem — not near a window or wall

Your first node needs to be close to where the internet enters your house (usually near a cable outlet or phone jack). Don't stretch that cable run across the house just to put the router in a "central" location — that can actually make things worse. The modem and first node should be right next to each other, connected with a short ethernet cable.

2

Second node in a central spot upstairs — not tucked in a corner

A hallway between the bedrooms is ideal. Avoid putting it in a back bedroom at the far end of the house — that only covers one room well. You want the second node positioned so it can reach all the rooms on that floor from the middle.

3

Never put nodes in closets, cabinets, or behind the TV

This sounds obvious, but we see it constantly. Signal has to pass through walls, doors, and furniture to get to your devices. A node in a cabinet or entertainment center is working at a fraction of its potential. Put it out in the open, on a shelf or an end table.

4

Wire the nodes together if you can (this is a big deal)

If you have ethernet ports in the walls — or you're willing to run a cable — connecting your nodes with ethernet instead of wirelessly nearly doubles performance. Every system on this list supports ethernet backhaul. If there's no ethernet but there is a coax outlet upstairs, MoCA adapters let you run the backhaul over your existing TV cable instead.

5

Turn off your ISP router's Wi-Fi

If your ISP (Spectrum, Frontier, etc.) gave you a router/modem combo, you need to disable its Wi-Fi or put it in "bridge mode." Running two routers at once causes what's called double-NAT — it creates conflicts that slow things down and break certain apps and devices. Call your ISP and ask them to put your gateway in bridge mode. Most will do it in a few minutes over the phone.

Don't skip step 5. Running your new mesh system behind your ISP's router is one of the most common mistakes we see. The double-NAT it creates makes everything noticeably slower and causes weird issues with smart home devices, gaming, and video calls.
